

### Module 2: Artificial Neural Networks and Classification Performance

**1. Biological vs. Artificial Neurons**
A biological neuron consists of dendrites (receivers), a cell body or soma (processor), and an axon (transmitter). Signals cross between neurons at junctions called synapses. An artificial neuron (or computational unit) mimics this by taking multiple inputs, multiplying them by weights, summing them with a bias, and applying an activation function.

**2. McCulloch-Pitts (M-P) Model and Perceptron**

* **McCulloch-Pitts Model:** The earliest mathematical model of a neuron (1943). It acts as a linear threshold gate; if the weighted sum of inputs exceeds a threshold, the neuron fires (output = 1).
* **Perceptron:** A single-layer model that can learn to classify linearly separable patterns.
* **Training Rule:** Weights are updated based on the error between the actual and predicted output: $w = w + \eta(actual - predicted)x$, where $\eta$ is the learning rate.
* **Boolean Implementation:** Perceptrons can implement AND, OR, and NOT functions, but they cannot handle non-linearly separable problems like XOR.



**3. Multilayer Perceptrons (MLP) and Backpropagation**
To solve non-linear problems like XOR, MLPs organize neurons into an input layer, one or more hidden layers, and an output layer.

* **Backpropagation:** The fundamental algorithm for training MLPs. It uses the chain rule of calculus to compute the gradient of the loss function with respect to weights, propagating the error backward to update them and minimize the overall loss.

**4. Resampling and Cross-Validation**
To ensure a model generalizes well, resampling methods like **K-Fold Cross Validation** are used. The dataset is split into *k* subsets; the model is trained on *k-1* subsets and validated on the remaining subset, repeating this process *k* times until every subset has been used for validation once.

**5. Measuring Classifier Performance**
Classification performance is evaluated beyond simple accuracy, which can be misleading for imbalanced datasets.

* **Precision:** Proportion of true positive predictions among all positive predictions ($TP / (TP + FP)$).
* **Recall (Sensitivity):** Proportion of actual positive instances correctly identified ($TP / (TP + FN)$).
* **F1 Score:** The harmonic mean of Precision and Recall, providing a balanced measure: $F1 = 2 \times \frac{Precision \times Recall}{Precision + Recall}$.
* **ROC Curve and AUC:** The Receiver Operating Characteristic curve plots the True Positive Rate against the False Positive Rate at various thresholds. The **Area Under Curve (AUC)** quantifies the model's discriminative power; an AUC of 1 is a perfect model, while 0.5 represents random guessing.
